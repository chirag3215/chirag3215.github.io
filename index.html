Networking Study Data Pipeline

1,917 Total Entries
1,027 Participants
6 Days of Data

Background

Designed and built an AI-powered OCR pipeline to process a large-scale networking study at UCSD. The pipeline extracted, cleaned, and categorized text from 1,917 participant screenshots — work that would have taken ~60 hours manually was reduced to under 2 hours.

Methodology

Participants were recruited via SONA, UCSD's research portal and instructed to screenshot their LinkedIn/email outreach messages. Each file was labeled with a unique participant ID for anonymity.

The pipeline used AI-based OCR to scan each screenshot, extract the message text, and automatically categorize entries by script adherence, platform, contact gender, and sender gender. The study's research question — whether sender gender affects response rates when message content is identical — served as the use case the pipeline was built to answer.

Key Improvements Identified

Future iterations should collect direct text input instead of screenshots to reduce OCR noise, add manual oversight for entries with complex backgrounds or overlapping text, and refactor the pipeline to accept varied file formats rather than a single fixed structure — making it reusable across different studies.

Sunlight Exposure & Overall Wellbeing

13 Participants
α = .896 Internal Reliability
r = .883 Convergent Validity

Background

As people spend increasing time indoors, sunlight exposure is decreasing. Research links morning light to cortisol regulation (Huberman, 2021), elevated serotonin (Boubekri et al., 1991), and improved immune response (Skobowiat et al., 2017). This study tested whether a simple daily sunlight routine benefits wellbeing.

Methodology

A randomized controlled experiment using convenience sampling. A first survey randomly assigned participants to either view morning sunlight or brush teeth daily for one week. A follow-up survey used a 15-item Likert scale validated against the Oxford Happiness Questionnaire (Hills & Argyle, 2002) to measure wellbeing.

Results

Average wellbeing scores were approximately 4/7 in both conditions (p = .88), indicating no statistically significant effect. The small sample size (n=13 completions), one-week duration, and high participant drop-off between surveys likely limited statistical power.

Future Directions

Future studies should measure wellbeing delta rather than mean, offer participation incentives to reduce attrition, and extend the study over multiple months to detect meaningful longitudinal change.

BCI Handwriting Primitives

16 Primitives Found
50K+ Characters Covered
CASIA Dataset Used

Background

Brain-computer interfaces for handwriting face a fundamental scaling problem: Chinese has over 50,000 characters, and training a classifier for each one individually is impractical. This project explored whether characters could instead be decomposed into a small, shared set of stroke primitives — reusable building blocks that appear across thousands of characters.

Methodology

Handwriting samples were sourced from the CASIA Online Handwriting Database, a widely-used benchmark in handwriting recognition research. Individual strokes were extracted, normalized for length and speed, and compared using Dynamic Time Warping (DTW) — a time-series similarity measure that accounts for natural variation in writing speed and style.

Strokes were then grouped using unsupervised clustering to identify recurring patterns. The resulting clusters were evaluated for consistency and coverage across the character set.

Results

The analysis identified 16 distinct stroke primitives that recur across the dataset. These primitives can be recombined to reconstruct the full range of characters, suggesting that a BCI system could learn just 16 patterns rather than tens of thousands — dramatically reducing training complexity and improving scalability.

Future Directions

Next steps include validating primitive coverage against a broader character set, testing whether BCI decoding accuracy improves when using primitive-based decomposition versus whole-character classification, and exploring how individual handwriting variation affects primitive stability across users.
